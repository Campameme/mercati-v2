# 02 · Quattro proposte di design

Quattro lenti indipendenti hanno proposto ciascuna una direzione UX. Per ogni proposta, 3 verifier adversariali (admin / vera semplicità / brand) hanno tentato di smontarla. Risultato qui sotto.

Le proposte NON sono mutuamente esclusive: la sintesi (`03-sintesi.md`) ne ruba elementi da ciascuna.

## Lente · Editorial

**Verifiche adversariali**: admin **9/10** · semplicità **5/10** · brand **5/10**

> IMercati feels like opening a slow Sunday magazine about Liguria. You land on a wide, almost cinematic photograph of crates of fava beans on stone, a single serif headline whispering "Imperia, mercato per mercato", and you scroll — not click — through the province. Each market is treated as a feature article: a hand-set wordmark, a dateline ("Ventimiglia, venerdì mattina, dal 1880"), a long paragraph of pure prose about who sells what and why, then quiet utility (orari, mappa, meteo) appearing only when the reader is ready. Type is generous Crimson Pro on cream #F4EEE2, with olive #5C6B47 used like a fountain-pen ink for marginalia. Photography is large, uncropped, never carouselled — one image breathes per fold. The interface barely interrupts; controls fade in with the scroll, calendar and map are inline figures with captions, and operators are introduced like profiled artisans, not listings. It feels read, not used — closer to Apartamento than to TripAdvisor — but every utilitarian answer (when, where, is it raining) is always one quiet sticky-rail click away.

**Principi**

- One image per fold. No carousels, no grids of thumbnails above the fold — a single full-bleed photograph carries the page, captioned in small caps like a magazine plate.
- Prose before chrome. Every market, operator and zone leads with a written introduction (60-180 words of editorial copy from a real author byline) before any filter, button, or data table appears.
- Serif headlines, sans utility. Crimson Pro for all narrative type; a single neutral sans (Inter) only for buttons, form fields, and tabular data — the typographic split signals 'reading mode' vs 'doing mode'.
- Utility is a sticky margin, not a hero. Quick actions (oggi è aperto?, indicazioni, meteo) live in a thin right-rail or sticky footer strip that follows the scroll, so the article remains the main column.
- Slow reveal over information density. Use scroll-triggered fade-ins, asymmetric two-column layouts, drop caps, pull quotes and end-marks. Nothing flashes, nothing badges, nothing pulses.

**Mosse brand**

- Commission or curate one signature photograph per zone (golden-hour, uncropped, at least 2400px wide) and treat it as inviolable — never overlay UI on top, never crop into a thumbnail, always full-bleed with a captioned plate number.
- Replace the generic stat cards on the admin dashboard with editorial pull-quotes: instead of 'Operatori attivi: 247' show 'Duecentoquarantasette banchi, undici comuni, una provincia. — La redazione'.
- Introduce a typographic system: Crimson Pro for all narrative, Inter only for forms/data, and a small-caps tracking-wide treatment for all metadata (datelines, captions, byline). Ban ALL CAPS uppercase except where small-caps.
- Give every zone its own identity: 5 curated palette accents (e.g. Ventimiglia = ochre, Sanremo = rose, Oneglia = sea-glass, Porto Maurizio = cobalt, Diano = terracotta) plus a unique ornamental glyph used as the end-mark for that zone's article.
- Add real bylines: every zone article, every operator profile, every avviso signed by a named editor or contributor ('a cura di Giulia M.' / 'testo di Marco P.'). Builds editorial trust and humanizes a civic site.
- Replace the 'Aderisci' CTA with a hand-set 'Diventa un volto di IMercati' invitation, framed as a letter from the editor — shifts onboarding from transactional to belonging.
- Publish a weekly editor's pick ('La scelta della settimana') prominent on the cover and in the colophon RSS, turning the site into a return-visit ritual rather than a one-off lookup tool.

**Trade-off dichiarati**

- Slower to find utilitarian answers: a tourist asking 'is the market open Saturday?' has to either find the sticky rail or scroll past prose — 1-2 extra seconds vs a chip grid.
- Heavier page weight: full-bleed photography per zone means real CDN cost and slower LCP on 3G; mitigated by AVIF + careful sizes but never as cheap as utility-first cards.
- Higher editorial burden: someone has to actually write 60-180 words per zone, per operator, per avviso. If copy isn't maintained the magazine feel collapses into 'lorem ipsum guida'.
- Less screen real estate for filters: search/category filters are deprioritized into sticky rails, which can hurt power users (e.g. journalists looking for all 'pesce' vendors).
- Calendar power-user features lose visibility: FullCalendar's month/week toggles become subtle, the magazine spread metaphor sacrifices some scannability for atmosphere.
- Mobile is harder: editorial layouts shine on tablet/desktop but a magazine spread on a 375px iPhone risks becoming a tall narrow stack of photos and prose — needs careful mobile-specific typography rules.
- Per-zone visual identity (5 palettes, 5 ornaments) increases QA surface and means every new zone added in the future needs a designer's touch, not just a slug + photo upload.
- Accessibility complexity: drop caps, decorative ornaments, italic prose at small sizes all need explicit a11y treatment (aria-hidden ornaments, sufficient contrast on cream, screen-reader friendly datelines).

**Trade-off dichiarati**

- Empty profiles kill the experience — if even 30% of operators lack portraits or stories, the homepage feels broken and the whole concept collapses. Requires a content-production sprint before launch and ongoing editorial enforcement.

- Discovery by market/zone becomes slower. A tourist arriving with intent 'what's at Crema today' has to scroll through faces instead of seeing a quick schedule grid. The /m/[slug] page mitigates but does not erase this friction.
- Calendar-first power users (people who want to see all the week's sessions at a glance) get a less efficient surface. The unified /calendario is good but no longer the homepage centerpiece.
- Operators with weak personal stories or shy of photos are systematically disadvantaged. The lens privileges photogenic, narrative-friendly artisans over functional vendors (e.g. someone selling bulk produce without a story).
- Search becomes harder for utilitarian queries ('mercato sabato Treviglio orari') — the editorial framing fights the SEO long-tail for transactional intent. Mitigate with strong structured data and metadata but accept the trade.
- Onboarding friction: requiring a portrait + 40-word first-person story before publish will reject some operators who would have signed up under a low-friction form. Adoption rate drops, average profile quality rises.
- Mobile scroll length explodes: one operator per screen means the homepage is potentially 50+ screens deep. Requires excellent infinite-scroll, filtering, and a 'jump to filter' UX or the experience feels endless rather than curated.

**Trade-off dichiarati**

- Print-first and document-first thinking adds engineering cost (real print.css, PDF export, revision tracking) that doesn't directly grow traffic.
- Animation, micro-interactions, and modern web 'craft' signals are intentionally absent. Designers reviewing the site may judge it as flat or dated.

- Two distinct visual systems (public civic + admin civic) still need to be built consistently; if execution slips, the site looks like an underfunded municipal portal instead of a confident one — which is exactly the failure mode the brief warns about ('government-issued').
